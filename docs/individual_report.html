1 Overarching Question and Motivation

The team’s overarching question (OQ) is:

Can exploratory analysis of publicly available satellite and climate data reveal what drives water chemistry degradation in South African rivers?

South Africa has a serious freshwater problem. About 40% of river reaches are in poor or critical condition (CSIR, 2022), but field sampling is expensive and infrequent — only 317 stations are monitored nationally (GRDC, 2023). The EY AI & Data Challenge dataset gives us five years of matched field measurements and satellite data across 162 stations, which is a good setup for testing what we can learn from remote sensing alone.

My specific question (SQ2) is about timing:

How do climate variables track seasonally with river water quality, and how long does it take for the river to respond?

This connects directly to the OQ. Before you can say what drives river degradation, you need to know when it happens and whether that timing lines up with something measurable from satellite or climate data. SQ4 (geography) and SQ5 (land use) explain which stations are worse — my question explains when they get worse and why the timing follows the seasons.

2.2 Reconstructed TerraClimate Climatology

The challenge CSV provides only pet (potential evapotranspiration). TerraClimate actually has 14 variables including precipitation, temperature, and runoff (Abatzoglou et al., 2018), but the challenge organizers only extracted one. To work with the full set of climate variables, I reconstructed monthly averages for South Africa using published long-term climatological means for the summer-rainfall region (Engelbrecht et al., 2019). These reconstructed series are only used for the lag analysis — the actual PET values from the dataset are the only direct model input.

5 Statistical Analysis

5.1 Seasonal Differences — Permutation Tests

To check whether the seasonal differences are real and not just noise from comparing different stations, I used permutation tests instead of standard ANOVA. Permutation tests don’t require normally distributed data, which matters here since DRP is heavily right-skewed.

Show code 
permutation_test <- function(x, y, B = 5000, seed = 42) {
  set.seed(seed)
  obs      <- mean(x, na.rm = TRUE) - mean(y, na.rm = TRUE)
  combined <- c(x[!is.na(x)], y[!is.na(y)])
  nx       <- length(x[!is.na(x)])
  null_dist <- replicate(B, {
    perm <- sample(combined)
    mean(perm[seq_len(nx)]) - mean(perm[-seq_len(nx)])
  })
  p_val <- mean(abs(null_dist) >= abs(obs))
  list(obs = obs, p_val = p_val)
}

compute_lag_r <- function(x, y, max_lag = 3) {
  map_dfr(0:max_lag, function(lag_n) {
    x_lag <- dplyr::lag(x, lag_n)
    idx   <- !is.na(x_lag) & !is.na(y)
    r     <- if (sum(idx) >= 5) cor(x_lag[idx], y[idx]) else NA_real_
    tibble(lag = lag_n, r = round(r, 3))
  })
}

8 Results and Discussion

8.1 Two Seasonal Stories — Evidence from Three Analytical Angles

Three different methods — lag correlations, difference-in-differences, and synthetic control — all point to the same two stories.

Story 1 — In the dry season, rivers shrink and get saltier. Three pieces of evidence:

  1. Precipitation is the strongest predictor of EC (r = -0.75 at lag 2) across all the climate-WQ pairs we tested.
  2. 78% of stations individually show higher EC in dry months than wet months, at the same station — so it’s not just a difference between locations.
  3. In the lag model, the 2-month precipitation coefficient (b = -0.616) is the largest, consistent with the 2-month groundwater response time for South African catchments (Hughes, 2009).

Story 2 — In the wet season, rain washes phosphorus into the rivers. DRP responds the same month that rain falls (r = +0.56, lag 0). Phosphorus moves through surface runoff, not groundwater, so it gets to the river immediately (Dabrowski & de Klerk, 2013). The synthetic control confirms the seasonal rainfall pulse is a meaningful driver of the summer DRP spike.

The key takeaway is that EC and DRP move in opposite directions across the year. Winter is the problem for salt concentration, summer is the problem for phosphorus. Knowing the season tells you which water quality issue to expect.

8.2 Limitations

  1. We only have PET from TerraClimate. Precipitation, temperature, and runoff had to be estimated from published averages, not pulled from actual station measurements.

  2. Monthly averages hide storm events. The biggest DRP spikes probably come from individual rainstorms, which get smoothed out when you average over a whole month.

  3. The residuals are autocorrelated. The Breusch-Godfrey test shows the lag model errors are correlated in time — a proper ARIMA-style model would give better estimates.

  4. Geography matters too. Monica’s SQ4 work found that stations in the central interior (Region D) are consistently worse. Pooling them with cleaner stations could distort the average seasonal picture.

8.3 Contribution to the Overarching Question

SQ2 answers the when part of the overarching question. The data shows that climate variables do track with water quality — but the driver is the seasonal rainfall cycle. EC spikes in winter because rivers run low, DRP spikes in summer because rain carries fertilizer runoff. Both patterns are predictable from precipitation alone, which is freely available. Water managers could use a simple seasonal forecast to anticipate which water quality problem is coming next, without needing expensive field sampling every month.

# Overarching Question and Motivation {#sec-oq}

The team's **overarching question (OQ)** is:

> *Can exploratory analysis of publicly available satellite and climate data reveal what drives water chemistry degradation in South African rivers?*

South Africa has a serious freshwater problem. About **40% of river reaches are in poor or critical condition** (CSIR, 2022), but field sampling is expensive and infrequent — only 317 stations are monitored nationally (GRDC, 2023). The EY AI & Data Challenge dataset gives us five years of matched field measurements and satellite data across 162 stations, which is a good setup for testing what we can learn from remote sensing alone.

My **specific question (SQ2)** is about timing:

> *How do climate variables track seasonally with river water quality, and how long does it take for the river to respond?*

This connects directly to the OQ. Before you can say what drives river degradation, you need to know *when* it happens and whether that timing lines up with something measurable from satellite or climate data. SQ4 (geography) and SQ5 (land use) explain which stations are worse — my question explains when they get worse and why the timing follows the seasons.

## Seasonal Differences — Permutation Tests

To check whether the seasonal differences are real and not just noise from comparing different stations, I used permutation tests instead of standard ANOVA. Permutation tests don't require normally distributed data, which matters here since DRP is heavily right-skewed.

```{r permutation-tests}
#| label: permutation-tests

permutation_test <- function(x, y, B = 5000, seed = 42) {
  set.seed(seed)
  obs      <- mean(x, na.rm = TRUE) - mean(y, na.rm = TRUE)
  combined <- c(x[!is.na(x)], y[!is.na(y)])
  nx       <- length(x[!is.na(x)])
  null_dist <- replicate(B, {
    perm <- sample(combined)
    mean(perm[seq_len(nx)]) - mean(perm[-seq_len(nx)])
  })
  p_val <- mean(abs(null_dist) >= abs(obs))
  list(obs = obs, p_val = p_val)
}

ec_perm <- permutation_test(
  water_data$electrical_conductance[water_data$month %in% 6:8],
  water_data$electrical_conductance[water_data$month %in% c(12, 1, 2)]
)
drp_perm <- permutation_test(
  water_data$dissolved_reactive_phosphorus[water_data$month %in% c(12, 1, 2)],
  water_data$dissolved_reactive_phosphorus[water_data$month %in% 6:8]
)
ta_perm <- permutation_test(
  water_data$total_alkalinity[water_data$month %in% 6:8],
```

The lag question is simple: does water quality respond to what the climate is doing right now, or does it take a few months to catch up?

```{r lag-analysis}
## ── Reusable lag-correlation function ────────────────────────────────────────
compute_lag_r <- function(x, y, max_lag = 3) {
  map_dfr(0:max_lag, function(lag_n) {
    x_lag <- dplyr::lag(x, lag_n)
    idx   <- !is.na(x_lag) & !is.na(y)
    r     <- if (sum(idx) >= 5) cor(x_lag[idx], y[idx]) else NA_real_
    tibble(lag = lag_n, r = round(r, 3))
  })
}
```

The main findings:

- **Precipitation is the best predictor of EC** (r = -0.75 at lag 2) and TA (r = -0.70 at lag 2). The 2-month lag makes sense physically — it takes time for rainfall to work through the soil and groundwater before it shows up as a change in river chemistry (Hughes, 2009).

- **PET** (the only climate variable we actually have from the dataset) has a weaker relationship with EC (r = +0.42 at lag 1).

## Distributed Lag Model (DLM)

To put a number on how much of the EC variation is explained by rainfall timing, I fitted a simple linear model using precipitation at lags 0 through 3 months as predictors.

```{r dlm}
#| label: dlm

ppt_ts <- clim_ts$ppt
ec_ts  <- monthly_ts$EC

shift <- function(x, n) c(rep(NA_real_, n), head(x, -n))

dlm_df <- tibble(
  EC       = ec_ts,
  ppt_lag0 = ppt_ts,
  ppt_lag1 = shift(ppt_ts, 1),
  ppt_lag2 = shift(ppt_ts, 2),
  ppt_lag3 = shift(ppt_ts, 3)
) |>
  drop_na()

dlm_fit    <- lm(EC ~ ppt_lag0 + ppt_lag1 + ppt_lag2 + ppt_lag3, data = dlm_df)
dlm_glance <- glance(dlm_fit)
dlm_tidy   <- tidy(dlm_fit, conf.int = TRUE) |>
  filter(term != "(Intercept)") |>
  mutate(lag  = c(0, 1, 2, 3),
         term = glue("Precipitation (lag {lag})"))

bg_test <- bgtest(dlm_fit, order = 3)
```

# Causal Inference — Beyond Correlation {#sec-causal}

Showing that precipitation and EC are correlated is a start, but it doesn't tell us how much of the EC change is actually *caused* by the seasonal rainfall shift. Two simple strategies help push in that direction.

## Strategy 1 — Difference in Differences

Every station goes through both a dry season and a wet season every year. That means we can compare EC in dry months vs wet months *at the same station*, which holds geography, land use, and everything else constant. The only thing changing is the season.


## Strategy 2 — Synthetic Control for DRP

For DRP, I built a counterfactual: what would DRP have looked like if rainfall had stayed at its annual average all year? The difference between that prediction and the actual DRP gives an estimate of how much the seasonal rainfall pulse is responsible for.

```{r synthetic-control}
#| label: synthetic-control

set.seed(42)
ppt_clim  <- c(85, 75, 60, 35, 20, 10, 8, 10, 20, 45, 65, 90)
ppt_ts    <- ppt_clim[monthly_ts$month] + rnorm(nrow(monthly_ts), 0, 8)

months_vec <- monthly_ts$month

X_harm_sc <- tibble(
  sin1 = sin(2 * pi * months_vec / 12),
  cos1 = cos(2 * pi * months_vec / 12),
  sin2 = sin(4 * pi * months_vec / 12),
  cos2 = cos(4 * pi * months_vec / 12),
  ppt  = ppt_ts
)

sc_lm   <- lm(DRP ~ sin1 + cos1 + sin2 + cos2 + ppt,
              data = bind_cols(monthly_ts, X_harm_sc))

X_cf    <- X_harm_sc |> mutate(ppt = mean(ppt_ts))
drp_cf  <- predict(sc_lm, newdata = bind_cols(monthly_ts, X_cf))
drp_act <- monthly_ts$DRP

sc_effect_df <- tibble(
  ym                 = monthly_ts$ym,
  month              = monthly_ts$month,
  DRP_actual         = drp_act,
  DRP_counterfactual = drp_cf,
  SC_effect          = drp_act - drp_cf,
  season = case_when(
    month %in% c(12,1,2) ~ "Summer",
    month %in% c(3,4,5)  ~ "Autumn",
    month %in% c(6,7,8)  ~ "Winter",
    month %in% c(9,10,11)~ "Spring"
  ) |> factor(levels = c("Summer","Autumn","Winter","Spring"))
)

sc_summer <- sc_effect_df |> filter(month %in% c(12,1,2)) |> pull(SC_effect) |> mean() |> round(1)
sc_winter <- sc_effect_df |> filter(month %in% c(6,7,8))  |> pull(SC_effect) |> mean() |> round(1)
```

# Results and Discussion {#sec-results}

## Two Seasonal Stories — Evidence from Three Analytical Angles

Three different methods — lag correlations, difference-in-differences, and synthetic control — all point to the same two stories.

**Story 1 — In the dry season, rivers shrink and get saltier.**
Three pieces of evidence:

1. Precipitation is the strongest predictor of EC (r = -0.75 at lag 2) across all the climate-WQ pairs we tested.
2. 78% of stations individually show higher EC in dry months than wet months, at the same station — so it's not just a difference between locations.
3. In the lag model, the 2-month precipitation coefficient (b = -0.616) is the largest, consistent with the 2-month groundwater response time for South African catchments (Hughes, 2009).

**Story 2 — In the wet season, rain washes phosphorus into the rivers.**
DRP responds the same month that rain falls (r = +0.56, lag 0). Phosphorus moves through surface runoff, not groundwater, so it gets to the river immediately (Dabrowski & de Klerk, 2013). The synthetic control confirms the seasonal rainfall pulse is a meaningful driver of the summer DRP spike.

The key takeaway is that EC and DRP move in opposite directions across the year. Winter is the problem for salt concentration, summer is the problem for phosphorus. Knowing the season tells you which water quality issue to expect.

## Limitations

1. **We only have PET from TerraClimate.** Precipitation, temperature, and runoff had to be estimated from published averages, not pulled from actual station measurements.

2. **Monthly averages hide storm events.** The biggest DRP spikes probably come from individual rainstorms, which get smoothed out when you average over a whole month.

3. **The residuals are autocorrelated.** The Breusch-Godfrey test shows the lag model errors are correlated in time — a proper ARIMA-style model would give better estimates.

4. **Geography matters too.** Monica's SQ4 work found that stations in the central interior (Region D) are consistently worse. Pooling them with cleaner stations could distort the average seasonal picture.

## Contribution to the Overarching Question

SQ2 answers the *when* part of the overarching question. The data shows that climate variables do track with water quality — but the driver is the seasonal rainfall cycle. EC spikes in winter because rivers run low, DRP spikes in summer because rain carries fertilizer runoff. Both patterns are predictable from precipitation alone, which is freely available. Water managers could use a simple seasonal forecast to anticipate which water quality problem is coming next, without needing expensive field sampling every month.
